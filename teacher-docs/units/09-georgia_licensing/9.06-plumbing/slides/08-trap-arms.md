# Trap Arms and Trap Weir

## Trap anatomy

```
Fixture drain
   │
   ↓
   ┌─────────────┐
   │             │
   │   Water     │  ← Water seal (≥ 2 inches deep)
   │   in trap   │
   │             │
   └──────┬──────┘
          │
          ↓ Trap arm (horizontal)
          │
          ↓
        Vent + drainage
```

The trap holds water that prevents sewer gas from rising into the building. The trap arm is the horizontal pipe between the trap and the vent.

## Trap weir

The trap weir is the top of the water surface in the trap — the level where water spills out into the trap arm during drainage.

```
Water flow during drainage:
   Fixture drain → Trap → Over weir → Trap arm → Vent + drainage
                                          ↑
                              Negative pressure here
                              could siphon trap dry
```

The vent prevents that negative pressure from siphoning the trap.

## Trap arm length limits

The trap arm must NOT exceed these lengths to prevent siphoning:

| Pipe size | Max trap arm length |
|---|---|
| 1-1/4 inch | 30 inches |
| 1-1/2 inch | 42 inches |
| 2 inch | 60 inches |
| 3 inch | 72 inches |
| 4 inch | 120 inches |

These are measured from the trap weir to the vent fitting (or wet-vented branch).

## Trap arm slope

Trap arm slopes downward toward the drain at:

```
1/4 inch per foot minimum
```

Same as drainage line slope.

## Slope and weir relationship

If trap arm slope is too steep:

- Water flows faster, creates siphoning effect
- Trap dries out
- Sewer gas enters

If trap arm slope is inadequate:

- Solids settle in trap arm
- Eventually clogs

## S-trap (prohibited)

An S-trap is two 90° fittings in series that look like an S:

```
   ↓
   │
   └─────────┐
             │
             ↓ Looks like S
   ┌─────────┘
   │
   ↓
```

S-traps are prohibited because:

- Tend to siphon themselves
- Hold solids
- Provide no proper venting path

Always use a P-trap (single 90° turn) with proper trap arm to a vent.

## Trap seal depth

```
Minimum water seal: 2 inches
Maximum water seal: 4 inches (some allow up to 4 for tubs with deep seal)
```

Too shallow = water evaporates faster, gas enters
Too deep = water displacement during use, gurgling

## Trap material

```
Common materials:
- PVC (most residential)
- ABS (similar)
- Copper (DWV grade, accessible installs)
- Brass (decorative, accessible bath sinks)
```

For under-sink installation, threaded plastic or compression PVC works fine.

## Cleanouts at traps

Larger traps (3 inch and larger) have integral cleanouts. Smaller residential traps:

```
- Lift trap up by hand to clean
- Or chemical drain cleaning for slow drains
- Or replace if heavily corroded
```

## Common trap issues

- Trap arm too long for pipe size → siphoning
- Trap missing (illegal — connects fixture drain directly to vent)
- S-trap installed
- Trap arm slope wrong (either way)
- Vent missing → trap can't hold water
- Trap seal evaporated (rarely-used floor drain)
  → Pour cup of water periodically

## Floor drains specifically

Floor drains often siphon dry from infrequent use:

```
Remedy: Pour 1 cup of water down every 1-2 weeks
   OR
Install trap primer (auto-feed device that refills the trap)
   OR  
Use deep-seal trap (more water reserve)
```
