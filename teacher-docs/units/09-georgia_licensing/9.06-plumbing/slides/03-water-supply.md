# Water Supply System Design

## What it includes

The water supply system delivers potable water from the service connection to each fixture:

- Service entrance (utility to building)
- Building water service
- Water heater
- Hot and cold distribution
- Branch lines to fixtures
- Stops at each fixture

## Service entrance

```
Utility main → Service tap → Curb stop → Building water service → 
Main shutoff inside building → Meter → House
```

Typical service entrance:

- Pipe material: copper, PEX, or HDPE
- Size: 3/4" or 1" for most single-family
- Depth: below frost line (12" minimum in Georgia for buried lines)
- Insulation: not strictly required in most of Georgia but good practice

## Pipe materials

| Material | Use |
|---|---|
| **Copper** | Most common; reliable; expensive |
| **PEX** | Flexible plastic; cheaper; growing acceptance |
| **CPVC** | Older plastic; declining use |
| **Galvanized** | Old construction; corrodes from inside out |
| **PEX-AL-PEX** | PEX with aluminum core for distribution mains |

For new residential construction in Georgia, PEX is the most common choice.

## Pipe sizing

Water supply sized to deliver adequate flow and pressure:

```
Fixture unit (FU) demand from each fixture:
   Kitchen sink: 1.4 FU
   Bath group (toilet+sink+tub): 5 FU
   Washing machine: 1.4 FU
   Hose bib: 2.5 FU
```

Sum fixture units in each pipe segment, look up required pipe size in code table.

## Standard residential sizing

For most single-family homes:

```
Service entrance: 3/4" or 1"
Main distribution: 3/4"
Branch to bath group: 3/4" or 1/2"
Branch to single fixture: 1/2"
   (Some short runs to single sink: 3/8" acceptable)
```

## Water pressure

```
Minimum allowable: 20 psi at point of use (60 psi typical at meter)
Maximum: 80 psi (over 80 → install pressure-reducing valve)
```

For homes with high incoming pressure (often 90-120 psi from utility):

- Pressure-reducing valve (PRV) at service entrance
- Set to 50-60 psi
- Some PRVs also include a bypass for higher flow at fire department demand

## Velocity limits

Code limits water velocity to prevent erosion and noise:

```
Cold water max velocity: 8 ft/sec
Hot water max velocity: 5 ft/sec
```

For practical purposes: don't undersize pipes thinking "smaller is fine."

## Branch and trunk

For multi-family or larger homes:

```
Trunk (main run): sized for total fixture units downstream
Branches: sized for downstream fixture units only
   
Riser to second floor: sized for total floor demand
```

## Cross-connection prevention

Required at:

```
- Hose bibs (vacuum breaker)
- Boiler feed lines (atmospheric vacuum breaker or RPZ)
- Lawn irrigation (PVB or RPZ)
- Commercial dishwashers (air gap)
- Process water systems (RPZ)
```

Cross-connections allow contaminated water to back-siphon into potable system. Prevention devices are mandatory.

## Insulation

Hot water lines should be insulated for:
- Energy efficiency (less heat loss in transit)
- Faster hot water at fixtures
- Reduced condensation on cold lines

Required by 2018 IECC for new residential construction.

## Common water supply errors

- Pipe sizing based on rule of thumb vs fixture-unit calculation
- No PRV when incoming pressure exceeds 80 psi
- Mixed materials creating galvanic corrosion
- Branch undersized for downstream demand
- Missing cross-connection protection
- Buried lines without proper depth
