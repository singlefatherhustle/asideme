# Refrigerant Handling and EPA 608

## What EPA 608 is

The EPA's Section 608 Technician Certification — required by federal law for anyone who maintains, services, or disposes of equipment containing regulated refrigerants. Without this certification, you cannot:

- Recover or recycle refrigerant
- Service air conditioning systems containing refrigerant
- Purchase regulated refrigerants

## The four certification types

| Type | Equipment |
|---|---|
| **I** | Small appliances (≤ 5 lbs charge) — window units, refrigerators |
| **II** | High-pressure systems — most residential AC, heat pumps |
| **III** | Low-pressure systems — large commercial chillers |
| **Universal** | All of the above |

Most HVAC technicians get Universal certification.

## How to certify

```
1. Study EPA 608 material (ACCA, Mainstream Engineering, etc.)
2. Take exam at approved testing organization
3. 100-question multiple choice
4. Pass: receive certification card (valid for life)
5. Cost: $40-150 depending on provider
```

The certification doesn't expire, but it's specific to the EPA regulation as written. If regulations change significantly, retake may be needed.

## Refrigerant types

| Refrigerant | Use | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **R-22** | Older AC equipment | Phase-out complete 2020 (still in service, replacement difficult) |
| **R-410A** | Most current residential/commercial | Standard for past decade, gradual phase-down ongoing |
| **R-32** | Newer high-efficiency equipment | Being introduced |
| **R-454B** | Next-generation low-GWP | New equipment 2024+ |
| **R-12** | Old refrigerators, automotive | Phase-out complete |

The phase-out is driven by environmental regulations (ozone depletion for old refrigerants, global warming potential for newer ones).

## Recovery procedures

When opening a refrigerant system:

1. **Recover** all refrigerant using approved recovery equipment
2. **Verify** evacuation level (specific vacuum required)
3. **Service** the equipment
4. **Recharge** with proper refrigerant
5. **Document** recovery and recharge quantities

Venting refrigerant to atmosphere is a federal violation. Civil penalties up to $44k per violation.

## Pressure tests

When servicing:

```
Standing vacuum test: ensure no leaks
   Pull vacuum to 500 microns
   Watch for 15 minutes — should hold steady
   If vacuum loses, find and fix leak before charging
```

## Leak detection

Methods:

- **Electronic leak detector**: sniffs for refrigerant in atmosphere
- **Soap bubbles**: traditional method for accessible joints
- **Ultraviolet dye**: introduced into system, glows under UV light
- **Pressurized test**: charge with nitrogen, test for leaks before refrigerant

## Common refrigerant handling errors

- Venting refrigerant during service (federal violation)
- Failing to recover before disposal (recyclers won't accept)
- Mixing refrigerant types
- Using wrong gauge set for the refrigerant
- Charging with wrong amount (too much or too little)
- Skipping vacuum test

## Customer signals

Symptoms of refrigerant issues:

- AC not cooling sufficiently → could be low charge
- Frosting on indoor coil → could be low charge or restricted airflow
- Hot air from registers → no cooling at all
- Higher electric bill → could indicate compressor running long due to low charge

Always check charge as part of seasonal service. Don't just "top off" — diagnose why charge is low first.
