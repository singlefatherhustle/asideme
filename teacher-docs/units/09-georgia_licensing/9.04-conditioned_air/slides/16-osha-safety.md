# OSHA Safety for HVAC

## Subpart relevance

HVAC work falls under OSHA 29 CFR Part 1926 (construction industry) for new installation and major retrofit work, and 29 CFR Part 1910 (general industry) for service and maintenance.

## Key safety topics for HVAC

| Topic | OSHA section |
|---|---|
| Personal Protective Equipment | 1926.95, 1910.132 |
| Hazardous communication (chemicals) | 1910.1200 |
| Lockout/Tagout | 1910.147 |
| Confined spaces | 1910.146 |
| Respiratory protection | 1910.134 |
| Electrical safety | 1910.331-335 |
| Ladder safety | 1926.1053 |
| Hand and power tools | 1926.300 |
| Welding and cutting | 1910.252 |

## High-frequency hazards in HVAC

### Refrigerant exposure

- **R-22**: low toxicity but displaces oxygen in confined spaces
- **R-410A**: similar; cold liquid causes frostbite
- **Ammonia (industrial)**: highly toxic

PPE: gloves, eye protection, ventilation. Adequate room ventilation when working with refrigerant.

### Electrical hazards

```
Most HVAC service involves:
   - 120 V or 240 V single-phase (residential)
   - 480 V three-phase (commercial)
   - Capacitors holding lethal charge after disconnect

Always:
   1. Verify power off with meter
   2. Discharge capacitors before service
   3. Use insulated tools
   4. Wear rubber gloves on energized work
```

### Cuts and lacerations

```
Sheet metal edges, copper tubing, brazing, knife blades
   → Wear cut-resistant gloves
   → Don't ride sheet metal edges with bare hands
   → Properly deburr cut tubing
```

### Burns

```
Brazing temperatures: 1100-1500°F
Soldering: 400-600°F
Refrigerant lines: very cold (frostbite from liquid)
   → Wear long sleeves when brazing
   → Keep first-aid burn ointment in truck
   → Allow lines to warm before touching
```

### Falls from height

```
Working on rooftops (commercial)
Working in attics (residential)
Working on ladders

Required:
   - Fall protection > 6 feet (commercial)
   - Proper ladder use (3-point contact)
   - Anchor harnesses to building structure
   - Cover floor openings
```

## Hazardous material communication

For chemicals used:

- Refrigerants (SDS available from manufacturer)
- Cleaning chemicals (acid cleaner for AC coils, etc.)
- Solvents (PVC primer)

Must have Safety Data Sheets (SDS) available on-site for each chemical.

## Lockout/Tagout

For service on energized equipment:

```
1. Identify energy sources (electrical, refrigerant, etc.)
2. Notify affected personnel
3. Shut down equipment
4. Isolate (disconnect, valve, etc.)
5. Apply lock and tag
6. Verify isolation (test with meter)
7. Perform work
8. Restore after work
```

Each worker uses their own lock; no shared / master locks.

## Confined space (commercial only)

When working in commercial HVAC equipment vaults, ducts, or similar:

- Pre-entry permit if it qualifies as "permit-required confined space"
- Atmospheric testing for oxygen and contaminants
- Continuous monitoring
- Standby person outside

This is mainly an industrial / commercial concern; residential rarely involves permit-required confined spaces.

## Common HVAC safety violations

- Working on energized equipment without PPE
- Not verifying power off before service
- Brazing without fire watch nearby
- Carrying brazed copper while still hot
- No fall protection on rooftop unit work
- Missing or expired SDS for chemicals on the truck
- Untrained workers handling refrigerant (EPA 608 required)
