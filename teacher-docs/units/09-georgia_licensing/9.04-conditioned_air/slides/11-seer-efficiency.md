# SEER and Efficiency Ratings

## What SEER measures

Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio — the cooling capacity (BTU) per watt of electricity consumed, measured over a typical cooling season.

```
SEER = Cooling output (BTU) / Electrical input (Wh) over a season
```

Higher SEER = more efficient = lower operating cost.

## Current minimum efficiency

Federal DOE minimums for new equipment (2023+ in southern states):

| Type | Minimum efficiency |
|---|---|
| Split-system AC | 14 SEER2 |
| Single-package AC | 14 SEER2 |
| Heat pumps (split) | 14.3 SEER2 / 7.5 HSPF2 |

Equipment sold for installation must meet these. Older units below minimum can stay but cannot be sold new.

## SEER vs SEER2 vs EER

| Metric | What it measures |
|---|---|
| **SEER** | Pre-2023 seasonal efficiency standard |
| **SEER2** | Current seasonal efficiency standard (more realistic test) |
| **EER** | Energy Efficiency Ratio at single 95°F outdoor / 80°F indoor — instantaneous |

```
Conversion approximate:
   SEER 16 ≈ SEER2 15
   SEER 18 ≈ SEER2 17
```

## HSPF and HSPF2

For heat pumps:

```
HSPF = Heating Seasonal Performance Factor (heating equivalent of SEER)
HSPF2 = Updated standard (replaces HSPF in 2023)
```

A heat pump with HSPF 8.0 means it produces ~8 BTU of heating per Wh of electricity over a typical heating season.

## What efficiency you choose

```
Builder grade (14 SEER2): cheapest, meets code
Mid-tier (16-18 SEER2): better efficiency, modest cost premium
High-efficiency (19-24 SEER2): variable-speed compressor, very expensive
```

The right choice depends on:

- Climate (hotter = pays off faster on cooling-side efficiency)
- Operating hours (more use = bigger savings from higher efficiency)
- Initial cost vs lifetime cost
- Customer's energy bill priorities

## Payback calculation

Simple example:

```
14 SEER2 unit: $5,000 installed, $850/yr operating cost
18 SEER2 unit: $6,500 installed, $700/yr operating cost

Cost difference: $1,500
Annual savings: $150
Payback: $1,500 / $150 = 10 years

If equipment lasts 15-20 years: payback works
If 18 SEER2 unit fails at 10 years: same cost as 14 SEER2
```

For high-end systems, payback periods vary widely. Sales tools include "payback calculators" but real returns depend on:

- Actual energy use
- Future electricity rates
- Maintenance differences

## What else affects efficiency

- **Proper sizing**: oversized unit short-cycles, hurts efficiency
- **Sealed ductwork**: ducts losing 20%+ of air = direct efficiency loss
- **Filter changes**: dirty filter reduces airflow, hurts efficiency
- **Proper charge**: undercharge or overcharge by 10% reduces efficiency 20%
- **Outdoor unit shading**: shade outdoor unit, runs cooler, slightly better SEER
- **Smart thermostat**: smarter operation reduces wasted runtime

## Common SEER errors

- Comparing SEER to SEER2 directly (different standards)
- Pricing based only on SEER, not actual customer benefit
- Selling high SEER on small systems where payback is too long
- Ignoring proper installation (which has bigger impact than +2 SEER)
- Treating SEER as efficiency under all conditions (it's a seasonal average)

## Energy Star labels

Equipment meeting EPA Energy Star criteria:

- Currently: SEER2 ≥ 15.2 for split systems
- Higher tiers for higher recognition
- Tax credits sometimes available for Energy Star-qualified equipment

Encourage customers to choose Energy Star equipment — improves their utility rebates and tax situation.
