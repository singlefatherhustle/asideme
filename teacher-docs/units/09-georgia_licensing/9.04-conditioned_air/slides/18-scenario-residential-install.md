# Scenario — Residential AC Replacement

## The setup

A homeowner calls — their 1990s 5-ton heat pump died. They want it replaced. They mention:

- 3-bedroom ranch, 2,400 sq ft
- Open floor plan
- Single-pane original windows (no upgrade planned)
- Average insulation, age-appropriate
- House is hot in summer afternoons, cool in mornings

## The diagnostic conversation

You ask:

```
1. What's the existing system rated? (5-ton outdoor unit)
2. How old is the ductwork? (original, 1990s)
3. What thermostat do they have? (basic, programmable)
4. Has anyone done a load calc since installation? (no)
5. What rooms are uncomfortable? (front rooms hot afternoon, master cool)
```

## The likely diagnosis

5-ton system in a 2,400 sq ft home is **almost certainly oversized**. The actual cooling load is probably 24,000-30,000 BTU/hr (2-2.5 tons).

Why it was oversized:

- 1990s sizing rule: "1 ton per 600 sq ft" (4 tons for 2,400 sqft, then bumped to 5 for "safety")
- No Manual J procedure used
- Manufacturer marked "good for up to 5-ton"
- Builder erred on the side of large

Symptoms of oversizing:

- House cools quickly (fast tonnage), then unit shuts off
- Humidity stays high (latent removal needs long run times)
- Customer says "it cools but feels clammy"
- Short cycling causes premature compressor failure

## The right replacement

```
1. Do a Manual J load calc on the home
   → Probably 24,000-30,000 BTU/hr cooling
   → Heating load varies by climate

2. Check ductwork
   → 1990s ducts probably oversized for the smaller new unit
   → Either accept (slight imbalance, manageable) or reduce
   → Consider duct sealing to improve efficiency

3. Choose equipment
   → 2.5-ton or 3-ton heat pump (sized to load)
   → Variable-speed or two-stage if upgrade budget allows
   → AHRI-matched coil

4. Pricing options
   → 14 SEER2 basic: $7,000-9,000 installed
   → 16 SEER2 mid-tier: $9,000-12,000
   → 18 SEER2 variable-speed: $12,000-15,000
```

## The customer conversation

```
"Your new system should be 2.5 to 3 tons, not 5. The old unit was 
oversized. This is actually good news — smaller system, smaller 
electric bill, better dehumidification."

(Customer skeptical because they want 'lots of cooling')

"Here's the math. A 5-ton at 50% capacity runs 30 minutes per hour 
to maintain temperature. The same 30 minutes per hour at proper 
size means longer run time, better dehumidification, more even 
temperatures, AND lower electric bill."
```

## Why this scenario matters

This is a very common service call in Georgia. The trap:

- Replacing oversized with oversized "because that's what was there"
- Salesperson is tempted to upsell to bigger unit
- Customer feels "more is better" without understanding HVAC

A licensed HVAC contractor should:

- Do the load calc (Manual J)
- Recommend correctly-sized equipment
- Educate the customer on why
- Document the load calc for warranty / inspection

## What can go wrong

If you replace with a 5-ton:
- Customer's energy bill stays roughly the same
- Humidity issues continue
- Premature failure (short cycling)
- Eventual callback when customer realizes the issue

If you replace with 2.5-ton without explanation:
- Customer feels "you took my cooling away"
- Negative reviews
- Possible warranty disputes
- May be uncomfortable until balancing complete

## The right execution

```
1. Sell the load calc as part of the diagnosis
2. Recommend properly-sized equipment with clear explanation
3. Include sealing the ducts in scope (often necessary)
4. Commission the system properly (measure airflow, charge, etc.)
5. Document on commissioning report
6. Walk the customer through performance characteristics
   - "Slightly longer runtime is normal and intended"
   - "You'll feel more even temperature"
   - "Humidity will improve"
   - "You'll see lower electric bill next month"
```

## Common scenario errors

- Replacing oversized with oversized
- Failing to do Manual J
- Reusing old ductwork without sealing
- Skipping commissioning
- Not setting customer expectations about runtime patterns
- Selecting the cheapest equipment instead of right-sized
