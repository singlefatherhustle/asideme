# Qualifying Agent for Business Organizations

## What it means

Georgia distinguishes between:

- **Individual license**: held by a person
- **Business organization license**: held by an LLC, corporation, or partnership through a designated qualifying agent

An individual license does NOT authorize work on behalf of an LLC or corporation, **even if the individual owns the business**.

## The qualifying agent's role

A qualifying agent has **final approval authority** for:

- All construction work
- All business matters
- Contracts
- Contract performance
- Financial affairs

This is documented in an affidavit signed by an authorized agent of the business organization.

## The affidavit requirement

Department 553 rules require:

```
Affidavit content:
- Acknowledges the qualifying agent's authority
- Names the qualifying agent
- States the business organization name
- Signed by an authorized agent (typically a manager or officer)
- Notarized
```

The affidavit goes on file with GREC.

## Number of qualifying agents

A business organization engaged in residential or general contracting must have **at least one qualifying agent**.

## General contractor specific requirement

For general contracting, the business must:

```
- Employ at least one currently licensed general contractor
- Who is actively engaged in the practice for the business on a full-time basis
- Who provides adequate supervision
- Who is responsible for the business's projects
```

This means the qualifying agent must:

- Be on the company payroll (W-2 or owner-equivalent)
- Not be merely a "qualifying name" without involvement
- Actually supervise the projects

## Common qualifying agent issues

### Borrowed license

Someone might offer to be the "qualifying agent" for a fee without actively supervising. This is illegal and can result in:

- License revocation
- Civil penalties
- Possible criminal charges for the company

### Lost qualifying agent

If the qualifying agent leaves the company:

```
1. Notify GREC immediately
2. Cannot continue contracting work
3. Must designate a new qualifying agent within timeframe (varies)
4. Business operates without active license until resolved
```

### Failure to supervise

If a qualifying agent isn't actually supervising projects:

- Inspector callouts may trigger investigation
- Customer complaints about quality may surface
- GREC can revoke the qualifying agent's status

## Real-life scenario

> A contractor has an individual residential-basic license but wants to pull permits and contract through a new LLC.

The individual license alone is NOT enough. Georgia treats business-organization practice differently:

```
Required:
1. The LLC is licensed through a qualifying agent
2. The individual is named as the qualifying agent
3. Affidavit on file with GREC
4. The LLC is registered with the secretary of state
5. All work is done in the LLC's name
6. Bonding and insurance in the LLC's name
```

## Real-life scenario (continued)

> A general contracting firm's qualifying agent leaves the company but the company keeps signing contracts and pulling permits using the old license number.

This creates a licensing problem:

- The business is no longer properly licensed
- All work performed during this period may be "unlicensed work"
- Customers can refuse payment or sue for return of payments
- GREC may impose fines or revoke remaining licenses

## Common qualifying agent errors

- Operating an LLC under an individual license
- "Borrowing" a license without actual supervision
- Not notifying GREC when qualifying agent leaves
- Failure to maintain affidavit on file
- Multiple businesses claiming the same qualifying agent who can't actually supervise all of them
