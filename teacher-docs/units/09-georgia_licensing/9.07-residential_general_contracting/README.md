## License Categories, Money Requirements, and Exam Formats

The Georgia residential/general contractor structure is where candidates lose points by mixing up scope, finance, and exam path.

### Financial responsibility + insurance

| License | Financial responsibility | General liability | Workers' comp | Contract cap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Residential Basic** | $25,000 by listed proof methods | $300,000 | If required | No cap |
| **Residential Light Commercial** | $25,000 (same methods) | $500,000 | If required | No cap |
| **General Contractor** | **$150,000** minimum net worth | $500,000 | If required | No cap |
| **General Contractor Limited Tier** | **$25,000** minimum net worth | $500,000 | If required | **$1,000,000** cap on any contract |

### Georgia Business & Law exam

Common to residential and general contractors. Per the PSI bulletin:
- **50 questions**
- **120 minutes**

## Qualifying Agents, Business Organizations, and Supervision

Georgia's contractor rules are very serious about the difference between an **individual license** and a **qualifying-agent license for a business organization**.

- An **individual license** does **not** authorize work on behalf of LLCs or corporations, **even if the individual owns the business**.
- For a business organization, the **qualifying agent** must have **final approval authority** for all construction work and all business matters, including contracts, contract performance, and financial affairs.
- The Department 553 rules require an affidavit to that effect, signed by an authorized agent of the business organization.
- A business organization engaged in residential or general contracting must have **at least one qualifying agent**.
- For general contracting, the business must employ at least one currently licensed general contractor who is **actually engaged in the practice for the business on a full-time basis**, provides **adequate supervision**, and is **responsible for the business's projects**.

In real life: a company cannot "borrow" a license casually and cannot operate legally after losing its qualifying agent without fixing the licensing problem.

## Codes, OSHA, and Project Coordination

The 2020 PSI bulletin still ties the residential exams to **2018 IRC/IBC** and older energy-code references, while DCA's 2026 adopted code page now shows **2024 ICC** families and Georgia amendments. **Two-layered exam-prep strategy:**

- Use the PSI/reference list **for testability**.
- Use DCA's current codes **for real-life work, permit coordination, and inspection expectations**.

OSHA 29 CFR Part 1926 appears in both the contractor PSI bulletin and the trade reference lists — **safety is not a side topic; it is part of the tested core**.

### Practical project coordination workflow

```
1. Confirm license class and scope
2. Estimate and bid
3. Contract execution
4. Permit / code planning
5. Schedule subs and order materials
6. Coordinate specialty trades
7. Site safety / OSHA compliance
8. Inspections and punch work
9. Closeout, lien-waiver, final billing, records
```

That workflow maps closely to the Business & Law outline and the contractor-license structure.

## Sample Exam-Style Questions

**Q:** What is the contract-value cap for a Georgia General Contractor Limited Tier license?
**A:** The financial limit for any contract is **$1,000,000**.

**Q:** What net worth must an applicant affirm for a Georgia General Contractor license?
**A:** **$150,000** minimum net worth.

**Q:** What reference is specifically named for the Georgia Business & Law exam?
**A:** The **Georgia-NASCLA Contractors Guide to Business, Law and Project Management**.

**Q:** If a candidate fails a residential or general-contractor exam twice, what happens?
**A:** Georgia requires the candidate to wait **at least one year** after the last exam before submitting a new application for a third or later attempt.

## Real-Life Scenario Questions

**Scenario:** A contractor has an individual residential-basic license but wants to pull permits and contract through a new LLC.
**Answer:** The individual license alone is **not enough**. Georgia treats business-organization practice differently; the business must be licensed through a **qualifying agent** with the required authority and affidavit.

**Scenario:** A general contracting firm's qualifying agent leaves the company but the company keeps signing contracts and pulling permits using the old license number.
**Answer:** That **creates a licensing problem**. Georgia rules require the business organization to have at least one valid qualifying agent and make qualifying agents responsible for supervision, project responsibility, and business matters during their period of service.
