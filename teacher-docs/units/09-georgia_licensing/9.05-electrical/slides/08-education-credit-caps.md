# Education Credit and the One-Year Cap

## Two distinct things

Don't confuse these:

| Thing | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Pre-license education** | Credit toward the 4-year experience requirement |
| **Continuing education (CE)** | Hours required for license renewal |

This slide covers pre-license. CE is a separate topic.

## Pre-license education credit

Formal post-secondary electrical education counts as **secondary experience**, credited at **50%**, capped at **1 year** total credit toward the 4-year requirement.

```
Accepted programs:
- Two-year associate degree (Electrical Technology, Industrial Electrical)
- Vocational / technical school certificate (Electrical Construction)
- Apprenticeship classroom hours
- Military technical school (MOS-dependent)
```

## The math

A typical AAS Electrical Technology degree:

- 2 years full-time school
- → Counts as 2 years of secondary experience
- → 2 × 50% = 1 year of credit
- → Hits the cap immediately

So a freshly graduated AAS holder needs **3 more years of primary field experience** to qualify.

## Programs that don't auto-qualify

The board reviews on a case-by-case basis. Programs that may struggle:

- Online-only courses with no hands-on component
- Programs with electrical content but a different primary focus (e.g., a general construction management degree that has 1-2 electrical classes)
- Hobbyist certifications (HAM radio, home theater)

If unsure, contact the board *before* paying the application fee — they'll let you know whether your program will be accepted.

## Apprenticeship paths

Formal IBEW (International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers) or IEC (Independent Electrical Contractors) apprenticeships:

- Typically 4-5 years
- Combine field experience + classroom instruction
- Classroom hours count as secondary (capped at 1 year)
- Field hours count as primary

A completed 4-year IBEW apprenticeship often by itself satisfies the experience requirement when properly documented.

## The bottom line

You cannot test your way out of field experience in Georgia. Even the best electrical education program only gets you 1 year toward the 4-year cap. The remaining 3 years must be hands-on installation work documented under a licensed contractor.
