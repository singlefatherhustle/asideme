# GFCI Requirements (NEC 210.8)

## What GFCI protects

A ground-fault circuit interrupter monitors imbalance between hot and neutral. When current "leaks" to ground (e.g., through a person), it trips at **4-6 milliamps** — fast enough to prevent electrocution.

## Where GFCI is required — dwellings (210.8(A))

**All 125 V, 15 A and 20 A receptacles** in:

```
- Bathrooms
- Garages
- Outdoors (with limited exceptions for de-icing equipment)
- Crawl spaces at or below grade
- Unfinished basements
- Kitchens — receptacles serving countertops
- Sinks — within 6 ft of any sink
- Boathouses
- Bathtub / shower areas
- Laundry areas
- Indoor damp / wet locations
- Garages, accessory buildings
```

## Where GFCI is required — non-dwellings (210.8(B))

```
- Bathrooms (all rec'ps)
- Kitchens — countertops + within 6 ft of sink
- Rooftops
- Outdoors
- Sinks — within 6 ft
- Indoor wet locations
- Locker rooms with showers
- Garages
- Crawl spaces, unfinished basements
- Boathouses
```

## 2023 NEC expansion

The 2023 NEC pushed GFCI coverage into more locations:

- Dwelling outdoor receptacles for heating cables, etc.
- Indoor damp/wet basement areas
- Receptacles for sump pumps and well pumps
- Receptacles for dishwashers

Georgia adopts the 2023 NEC effective Jan 1, 2026 — these expansions become enforceable then.

## Where GFCI is NOT required

- Behind a refrigerator/freezer (older NEC permitted; 2023 has limits)
- Hard-wired equipment with no plug
- Industrial machinery (use 60 mA Class B for equipment protection)
- Receptacles in unfinished attic (not at or below grade)

## GFCI types

| Type | Protects |
|---|---|
| **Class A** | People; trips at 4-6 mA |
| **Class B** | Equipment; trips at 20 mA (industrial) |
| **GFCI receptacle** | Itself + downstream wired through LINE/LOAD |
| **GFCI breaker** | Entire circuit from the panel |

## Common GFCI installation rules

- Test the GFCI monthly (manufacturer recommendation, often signaled by a label)
- GFCI tripping nuisance can indicate wiring issues — diagnose, don't replace blindly
- GFCI breakers cost more but cover entire circuits; receptacles are cheaper per device
- Cannot share a neutral between two GFCI receptacles (causes imbalance)
