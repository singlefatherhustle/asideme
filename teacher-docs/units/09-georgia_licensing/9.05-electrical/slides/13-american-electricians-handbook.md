# American Electricians' Handbook

## What it is

The American Electricians' Handbook (Croft / Summers, McGraw-Hill) is one of the oldest practical references in the trade — first published in 1913, currently in its 17th edition. It is on Georgia's approved reference list for the contractor exam.

## What it covers

A comprehensive desk reference, organized into ~30 sections:

| Section | Topic |
|---|---|
| 1 | Fundamentals of electricity |
| 2-4 | Wiring design and protection |
| 5-7 | Wiring methods, materials, and equipment |
| 8-9 | Lighting design |
| 10-12 | Motors, generators, transformers |
| 13-15 | Power transmission and distribution |
| 16-18 | Control circuits and instrumentation |
| 19-21 | Special systems (signal, fire alarm, security) |
| 22-24 | Industrial applications |
| 25-27 | Renewable energy and storage |
| 28-30 | Reference tables and formulas |

## Why it's useful on the exam

The handbook fills gaps the NEC doesn't:

- **Worked examples** of common calculations
- **Industry-standard practices** that aren't in the code but are expected
- **Formulas** for voltage drop, three-phase math, power factor correction
- **Background theory** the NEC assumes you already know

When a question asks about a calculation method, the handbook usually has a step-by-step example. When the NEC says "shall be calculated per industry standard practice," the handbook tells you what that practice is.

## Tabbing recommendation

Heavy tabs on these sections for the exam:

- Section 1 (theory + fundamentals)
- Section 5 (wiring methods overview)
- Section 10 (motors)
- Section 28 (formulas and conversions)
- Reference tables in the back

## Limits

The handbook is a **reference**, not a code. Where the handbook and NEC disagree, the NEC wins because the code has the force of law. Use the handbook for context and worked examples, not as your code authority.
