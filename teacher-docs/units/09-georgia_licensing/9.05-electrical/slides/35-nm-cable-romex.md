# NM Cable (Romex)

## What it is

Non-metallic sheathed cable, commonly called by the trade name **Romex**. The standard wiring method for residential branch circuits in dry locations. Governed by NEC **Article 334**.

## Constructions

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **NM-B** | Dry locations, 90°C insulation (ampacity rated 60°C for terminations) |
| **NMC** | Dry, damp, or corrosive locations |
| **NMS** | Multi-conductor with signaling / data |

NM-B is by far the most common in modern residential construction.

## Allowed uses — 334.10

- Dwelling units of any height (changed in 2014; previously 3 stories)
- Multifamily dwellings of any height (no longer limited)
- Other structures permitted by 334.10
- Type V (wood-frame) construction primarily
- Concealed within walls, ceilings, attics

## Prohibited uses — 334.12

- Wet locations (rain, in concrete, below grade without sleeve)
- As service entrance cable
- In commercial garages, theaters, hazardous (classified) locations
- Embedded in poured concrete or aggregate
- Exposed where subject to physical damage

## Securing and supporting — 334.30

- Within **12 inches** of every cabinet, box, or fitting (or 8 inches for boxes without cable clamps)
- Then at least every **4.5 feet** along the run
- Through bored holes in studs, joists — no specific support needed at each penetration
- Stapled flat against framing, never bent at sharp angle

## Bore-hole rule (300.4(A))

When NM cable passes through bored holes in framing:

- Hole must be ≥ 1-1/4 inches from the edge of the framing member
- If less, install a 1/16" steel plate over the wood face to protect the cable

## Smaller-than-#14 prohibition

You cannot install NM cable smaller than 14 AWG for the ungrounded/grounded conductors (smaller is permitted only for the equipment grounding conductor).

## Bundling derating

When 4 or more current-carrying conductors are bundled in NM cable runs longer than 24 inches without space:

- Apply ampacity adjustment factor per Table 310.15(C)(1)
- Common scenario: many homerun cables stacked against attic insulation

## Common installation errors

- Stapling through the sheath (damage to insulation)
- Sharp bends (less than 5× cable diameter)
- Running through air gaps in fire-rated assemblies without listed firestop
- Splicing outside of approved enclosures
- Failing to use protective plates on shallow bores
