# Sample Exam Question — GFCI Requirements

## The question

> In a new residential dwelling, identify which of the following 120V receptacle locations require GFCI protection per NEC 210.8(A):
>
> 1. Bathroom
> 2. Hallway
> 3. Kitchen island countertop
> 4. Master bedroom
> 5. Outdoor on a covered patio
> 6. Unfinished basement
> 7. Within 6 feet of a kitchen sink
> 8. Garage
> 9. Living room wall

## Answer for each

**Required GFCI:**

```
1. Bathroom                    ✓ (any rec'p in bathroom)
3. Kitchen island countertop   ✓ (kitchen countertop rec'ps)
5. Outdoor on covered patio    ✓ (all outdoor rec'ps)
6. Unfinished basement         ✓ (specific call-out)
7. Within 6 ft of kitchen sink ✓ (sink-proximity rule)
8. Garage                      ✓ (all garage rec'ps)
```

**NOT required to be GFCI:**

```
2. Hallway          ✗ (not in the GFCI list)
4. Master bedroom   ✗ (AFCI is required here, but not GFCI)
9. Living room wall ✗ (AFCI is required, but not GFCI)
```

## The list to memorize for residential GFCI

NEC 210.8(A) requires GFCI for 125V single-phase 15A and 20A receptacles in:

```
- Bathrooms (all)
- Garages (all)
- Outdoors
- Crawl spaces at or below grade
- Unfinished basements
- Kitchen countertops
- Within 6 ft of any sink
- Boathouses
- Bathtub / shower areas
- Laundry areas
- Indoor damp / wet locations
- Indoor dishwasher receptacles (2023 NEC)
- Sump pump receptacles (2023 NEC)
```

The full list expanded in the 2023 NEC. The 2020 NEC has a smaller subset.

## Common confusion: GFCI vs AFCI

```
GFCI: Wet locations + sinks → people-protection (shock)
AFCI: Habitable rooms → fire-protection (arcing wiring)
```

Many locations require both (e.g., kitchen countertops require GFCI **and** the kitchen branch circuit requires AFCI — different protections, both needed).

## The 6-foot rule

The "within 6 feet of a sink" rule is broadly applied. Examples:

- Kitchen sink ✓
- Bathroom sink ✓
- Bar sink ✓
- Wet bar ✓
- Laundry sink ✓
- Utility sink ✓

If a sink is present anywhere, any rec'p within 6 ft (measured around obstructions) needs GFCI.

## Exam takeaway

```
- Memorize the GFCI list as a checklist
- Understand the 6-ft sink rule
- Know that AFCI ≠ GFCI (different rooms, different protection)
- The 2023 NEC list is broader than 2020 — verify the edition you're tested on
```

## Real-world note

Most modern dwellings have **GFCI breakers at the panel** for entire circuits rather than GFCI receptacles, which is cleaner installation. Either method is code-compliant. The trade-off is cost vs convenience: GFCI receptacles are cheaper but install at each location; GFCI breakers cost more per circuit but cover everything downstream.
