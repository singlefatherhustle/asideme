# Conductor Color Codes

## NEC-required colors

The NEC specifies colors for two conductors:

| Conductor | Required color |
|---|---|
| **Grounded (neutral)** | White or gray |
| **Equipment grounding (EGC)** | Green, green w/ yellow stripe, or bare |

For ungrounded (hot) conductors, the NEC does NOT mandate specific colors except:

- Travelers in switching systems cannot use white or gray
- High-leg in delta systems must be **orange**

## Standard industry conventions

While not strictly required, the industry uses consistent color schemes by voltage system:

### 120/240 V single-phase
```
Hot 1:       Black
Hot 2:       Red
Neutral:     White
Ground:      Green / bare
```

### 120/208 V three-phase (Wye)
```
Phase A:     Black
Phase B:     Red
Phase C:     Blue
Neutral:     White
Ground:      Green
```

### 277/480 V three-phase (Wye)
```
Phase A:     Brown
Phase B:     Orange
Phase C:     Yellow
Neutral:     Gray
Ground:      Green
```

### 120/240 V three-phase (Delta) — high-leg
```
Phase A:     Black
Phase B (high-leg):  ORANGE  ← required by NEC
Phase C:     Blue
Neutral:     White
Ground:      Green
```

## High-leg rule — 110.15

The conductor with the higher voltage to ground (the "wild leg" in a 240 V delta system) must be identified by an orange color OR by other effective means, AT EVERY POINT where the system is exposed (panels, junction boxes, etc.).

## Re-identification rules

- Conductors **larger than 6 AWG** in a feeder can be re-identified at termination with tape, paint, or other means
- Conductors **6 AWG or smaller** must be the right color from manufacturer (factory-colored)
- Travelers in 3-way / 4-way switching are commonly red and blue, not white

## Common exam questions

- "Which conductor is the high-leg?" → orange in a 120/240 V delta
- "Acceptable colors for the EGC?" → green, green/yellow, bare
- "Can you re-identify a #10 black conductor as a neutral?" → No, too small for re-identification
