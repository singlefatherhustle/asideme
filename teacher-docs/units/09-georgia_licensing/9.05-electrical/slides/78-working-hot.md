# Working Hot — When and How

## The OSHA rule

29 CFR 1910.333 and 1926.416 require workers to **de-energize** equipment before service. Working on energized equipment ("working hot") is only allowed if:

1. **Continuity of service** would create unacceptable risk (e.g., hospital life-support)
2. **De-energization introduces additional hazards** (e.g., shutdown causes other failures)
3. **Infeasibility** (rare; specific documentation required)

In all three cases, the employer must:

- Document the justification
- Develop a written energized work procedure
- Provide proper PPE
- Have only qualified persons perform the work

## Energized work permit

When working hot, a written Energized Work Permit is required, including:

- Description of the work
- Justification for not de-energizing
- Identification of the qualified person(s)
- Arc flash and shock hazard analysis
- PPE specified
- Boundary distances
- Approval signatures from supervision

The permit must be on-site during the work and retained per company policy.

## What "qualified person" means

NFPA 70E definition. A qualified person:

- Is trained and skilled in the construction and operation of the equipment
- Knows the hazards involved
- Can identify exposed live parts from non-live
- Knows the approach boundaries and PPE for the voltage
- Can use specific protective techniques required (insulated tools, etc.)

Mere certification or licensing doesn't make someone "qualified" for a specific task; site-specific knowledge matters.

## Insulated tools

For working on energized equipment up to 1,000 V:

- Tools must be **listed** and rated for the voltage
- Must be insulated to at least the system voltage
- Visual inspection before each use
- Voltage-rated tools should be inspected for cracks, cuts, or insulation breakdown

## Tested-as-de-energized rule

If you intend to work de-energized, you must:

1. Open the disconnect / breaker
2. Apply LOTO
3. **Verify zero voltage** with a calibrated meter at the working point — both phase-to-phase AND phase-to-ground
4. Re-test the meter on a known live source after testing to confirm it still works

Skipping step 3 has killed countless electricians. Always test.

## When working hot is justified

Realistic examples:

- Hospital UPS battery work (can't shut down patient care)
- Continuous-process manufacturing where shutdown costs more than safety margin
- Emergency response to active failures
- Specific small-scale work like adding a circuit to a panel with the main left on (a common, but legally questionable, residential practice)

## Common working-hot mistakes

- Working "just to take a measurement" without proper PPE
- Assuming a circuit is dead based on indicators (lights off, breaker off) without testing
- Working alone in energized work
- Using non-insulated tools because "it's a quick job"
- Skipping the documentation because no one will check
