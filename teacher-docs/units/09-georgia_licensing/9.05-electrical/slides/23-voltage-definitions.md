# Voltage Definitions and Classifications

## Voltage to ground vs nominal voltage

**Voltage to ground** = highest voltage between any hot conductor and ground.
**Nominal voltage** = the named voltage of the system (e.g., 240 V).

These can differ. In a 240 V single-phase system, voltage to ground is 120 V (since the neutral grounds the midpoint).

## Common system voltages

| System | Nominal | To ground |
|---|---|---|
| Residential single-phase | 240 V | 120 V |
| Light commercial 3-phase Wye | 208 V | 120 V |
| Commercial 3-phase Wye | 480 V | 277 V |
| 240 V Delta (3-phase) | 240 V | 240 V (B-phase) or 120 V (A,C) |
| Industrial | 600 V | varies |

## Why the distinction matters

The 3-foot/4-foot working space rule (110.26) uses **voltage to ground**, not nominal voltage. A 480 V three-phase Wye has 277 V to ground, so it falls in the 151–600 V row of the table.

## Low-voltage thresholds

| Term | Voltage range |
|---|---|
| **Extra-low voltage** | < 50 V |
| **Class 1, 2, 3 circuits** | NEC Article 725; limited energy circuits |
| **Power-limited fire alarm (PLFA)** | NEC Article 760 |
| **Communications circuits** | NEC Article 800; phone, data |

Below 50 V to ground, many NEC rules relax (e.g., GFCI not required for 24 V DC).

## High-voltage thresholds

| Term | Voltage range |
|---|---|
| **Medium voltage** | 600 V – 35 kV |
| **High voltage** | > 35 kV |

Medium-voltage work (commercial substation, large industrial primary) has dedicated NEC articles (490) and dramatically different working space rules.

## Common confusion

Candidates often mix up:

- 240 V single-phase (residential) → 120 V to ground
- 240 V three-phase delta → 240 V to ground on the high-leg, 120 V on the other two

Always determine voltage to ground from the system's actual configuration, not just the nameplate.
