# Out-of-State Licensees in Georgia

## The general rule

A person licensed in another state **cannot** simply practice real estate in Georgia. They must either:

1. Obtain a Georgia license through reciprocity or examination
2. Refer the transaction to a Georgia-licensed broker (cooperative agreement)

## Reciprocity options

Georgia offers reciprocity with several states. The list changes periodically; always verify current eligibility with GREC. Recent reciprocal states include:

- Florida
- Alabama
- Tennessee
- South Carolina
- North Carolina (limited)
- West Virginia (limited)

## Reciprocity application

A licensee from a reciprocal state may apply for a Georgia license by:

1. Submitting application + fee to GREC
2. Demonstrating current active license in the reciprocal state
3. Completing **Georgia-specific portion** of the licensing exam (some states require full exam)
4. Background check
5. Affiliation with Georgia broker

## Non-reciprocal states

A licensee from a non-reciprocal state must:

1. Complete Georgia pre-license education (full hours)
2. Pass full Georgia salesperson or broker exam
3. Submit application + fees
4. Background check
5. Find sponsoring Georgia broker

## The 60-day "snowbird" exception

Georgia allows a non-resident licensee to refer a client **once** without holding a Georgia license:

- The licensee must hold an active license in another state
- The referral is to a Georgia-licensed broker
- The referring licensee receives a referral fee only
- The actual transaction is handled by the Georgia broker

This is a narrow exception, intended for relocations. Repeated use is not permitted.

## Working under reciprocity

A reciprocal license holder must:

- Affiliate with a Georgia broker (cannot operate independently)
- Use the Georgia broker's office address
- Comply with Georgia advertising rules
- Take Georgia-specific CE
- Pay full Georgia renewal fees

## Brokerage of record for out-of-state work

If a Georgia client is being served by an out-of-state agent (e.g., for a property the client is selling in Florida), the Florida agent is the "selling agent" but must coordinate through their Florida broker. Georgia regulations don't intervene in out-of-state transactions.

## Cross-border commission splits

When a Georgia broker cooperates with an out-of-state broker on a transaction:

- The commission split is negotiated between brokers
- Each broker compensates their own agents per their internal agreement
- Both brokers must be licensed in their respective states
- The buyer/seller documentation reflects the appropriate broker for each side

## Common out-of-state errors

- Holding open houses in Georgia while only licensed elsewhere (illegal)
- Advertising Georgia properties as an out-of-state agent
- Receiving commissions directly from Georgia closings
- Marketing in Georgia under out-of-state license
- Attempting reciprocity from a non-listed state
