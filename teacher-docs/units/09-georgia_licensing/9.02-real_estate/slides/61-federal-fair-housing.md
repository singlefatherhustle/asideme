# Federal Fair Housing Act

## What it covers

The Federal Fair Housing Act of 1968 (Title VIII of the Civil Rights Act, amended 1988) prohibits discrimination in:

- Sale of housing
- Rental of housing
- Mortgage lending
- Advertising of housing
- Brokerage services

## The 7 protected classes (federal)

```
1. Race
2. Color
3. National Origin
4. Religion
5. Sex (including sexual harassment in housing)
6. Familial Status (children, families with minors)
7. Disability (physical or mental)
```

In 2024, sexual orientation and gender identity were added in interpretations under the "sex" classification per HUD guidance.

## Prohibited practices

### 1. Refuse to sell or rent

You cannot refuse to deal with someone because of their protected class. Even seemingly neutral reasons can be discriminatory:

```
"We don't take Section 8 vouchers" — okay generally
"We don't take Section 8 vouchers in this neighborhood" — discriminatory
```

### 2. Discriminate in terms or conditions

Same product, same terms. Cannot:

- Charge more rent based on protected class
- Require more onerous lease terms
- Provide different services to different groups
- Charge differential security deposits

### 3. Make discriminatory advertisements

The advertising itself cannot indicate preference:

```
ILLEGAL:
"Single white female preferred"
"Christian household"
"No children"
"Adult community" (unless 55+ housing exemption)

LEGAL:
"3-bedroom, 2-bath"
"Quiet neighborhood"
"Near public transit"
```

### 4. Steer

Directing buyers/renters toward or away from specific neighborhoods based on protected class:

```
ILLEGAL:
"You probably want to see houses in the East side — that's where 
 most of [your group] live."

LEGAL:
"What are your preferences for neighborhood characteristics? 
 Commute, schools, amenities?"
```

### 5. Block-busting

Inducing residents to sell by suggesting protected class members are moving in, with the implication that property values will decline.

```
ILLEGAL:
"You should sell now before more [group] move in and prices drop."
```

### 6. Redlining

Denying mortgage loans or insurance in certain neighborhoods based on protected class composition of the area.

## Exemptions

Limited situations where Fair Housing rules don't apply:

- **Owner-occupied 4-or-fewer-unit dwelling** (Mrs. Murphy exception): single owner-occupied unit can be excluded from familial status protection but NOT race/color/national origin protection
- **Single-family home sold by owner** without broker AND without discriminatory advertising: exempt from familial status
- **Religious organization housing** restricted to members of that religion: may be permissible
- **55+ senior community** that meets HUD requirements: can exclude families with minor children

These exemptions are narrow and require very specific qualifying conditions.

## Enforcement

Fair housing complaints can go to:

- HUD (Department of Housing and Urban Development)
- Department of Justice
- State enforcement agencies (GREC for Georgia license-related)
- Private lawsuit

Statute of limitations: 1 year for HUD complaint, 2 years for private suit.

Penalties:

- Compensatory damages (financial loss)
- Punitive damages
- Injunctive relief
- Civil penalties (up to $16k+ per first offense)
- License suspension or revocation for real estate professionals

## Common fair housing errors

- Using "family-friendly" or similar phrases that imply preference
- Showing different homes to different demographic groups based on assumption
- Refusing to show homes to clients with children
- Making jokes or comments that reveal bias
- Failing to advise client to consult lawyer when fair housing issues arise
- Not addressing fair housing in agency disclosure or training
