# Broker Scope and Authority

## What a broker can do

A Georgia real estate broker has full authority to:

- Operate a real estate brokerage independently
- Hire and supervise licensed salespersons and associate brokers
- Maintain trust accounts for earnest money and other client funds
- Sign contracts on behalf of the brokerage
- Set commission rates and policies
- Advertise services in their own name
- Receive commissions directly from closings
- Approve all listings, sales contracts, and transactions in the firm

## The supervisory responsibility

A broker is **legally responsible** for everything their salespersons and associate brokers do in the course of real estate business. Specifically:

- Trust account compliance
- Advertising compliance
- Agency disclosure compliance
- Anti-discrimination compliance
- License renewal of subordinate agents
- Continuing education tracking

If a salesperson commingles funds, the broker is on the hook for the violation — even if they didn't authorize it.

## Brokerage firm requirements

A brokerage operating in Georgia must:

- Be registered with GREC as a firm
- Designate one **Qualifying Broker** to oversee operations
- Maintain a physical office address
- Display the broker's license prominently
- Maintain proper trust accounts
- Comply with all advertising and disclosure rules

## Qualifying Broker

The qualifying broker:

- Is the legally responsible party for the firm
- Must be a licensed broker (not associate broker)
- Approves all trust account transactions (or delegates with documentation)
- Reviews advertisements before publication
- Conducts quarterly trust account reconciliation
- Reports any agent misconduct to GREC

## Salesperson supervision

A broker supervising salespersons must:

- Provide a written supervision policy
- Train salespersons in firm procedures
- Make timely review of contracts and disclosures
- Be available for consultation
- Maintain a written supervision log if challenged by GREC

A broker cannot legally supervise too many agents — Georgia doesn't set a hard limit, but "reasonable supervision" is a GREC test. Some firms have 50+ agents under one broker; others have 5. The standard is the broker's ability to actually supervise.

## Multi-licensee firm structure

```
Qualifying Broker
   ↓
   Brokers (associate)
   ↓
   Salespersons + Associate Brokers
   ↓
   Specific transactions
```

Larger firms add layers — managing brokers per office, designated supervisors for specific functions, etc. The supervision rule applies at every level.

## Common broker errors

- Operating without registered brokerage firm
- Letting the brokerage lapse while continuing to operate
- Failing to designate qualifying broker
- Allowing salespersons to advertise as if independent
- Inadequate supervision of new licensees
- Trust account violations not detected at quarterly review
