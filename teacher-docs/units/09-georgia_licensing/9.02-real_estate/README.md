# Georgia Real Estate Commission — Study Guide

**Unit:** Georgia Licensing
**Topic:** Real Estate (GREC)

---

## Governing Law and the Rules to Tab First

Georgia real-estate state-law study starts with a short "must-know" stack:

| Authority | What it governs | Why it matters on the exam |
|---|---|---|
| O.C.G.A. Title 43, Chapter 40 | Real estate brokers and salespersons | License authority, discipline, unfair practices, trust-fund duties |
| O.C.G.A. Title 10, Chapter 6A | Brokerage Relationships in Real Estate Transactions Act | Agency relationships and brokerage engagement duties |
| Rule 520-1-.06 | Brokerage relationships | Expiration dates, management agreements, written disclosure timing |
| Rule 520-1-.08 | Trust accounts and trust funds | Registration, deposit timing, broker handling, interest-bearing account conditions |
| Rule 520-1-.09 | Advertising | Permission to advertise, listing-firm disclosure, internet-ad requirements |
| Rule 520-1-.10 | Handling transactions | Presenting offers, license numbers, copies to signers, document retention, anti-falsification |
| Rule 520-1-.11 | Licensees acting as principals | Written broker notice and principal-disclosure rules |

The **highest-yield state-specific definitions and concepts**: a brokerage engagement, agency vs nonagency, dual agency disclosure, trust funds, designated trust/escrow account, licensee as principal, management agreement, and advertising "in the name of the firm." The exam often turns on a **single missing document, a late disclosure, or a wrong person holding earnest money**.

## What Georgia Practice Requires in the Real World

Georgia requires that each **exclusive brokerage agreement** fully state its terms and have a **definite expiration date**. The licensee who secures it must give each signer a true copy at the time of signing. GREC also prohibits **net brokerage engagements** — the broker must add the broker's fee so the client understands both the gross price and the broker's compensation. These are classic exam traps because the wrong answer usually "sounds practical" but misses the required writing or expiration date.

For **property management agreements**, Georgia is unusually specific. A written management agreement must:
- Identify the property
- State the terms of management
- Explain how and when income is remitted and statements are sent
- Specify how expenses are paid
- State compensation
- Say who holds security deposits / prepaid rents
- Include effective and termination dates
- Contain termination terms
- Include signatures

If you see an exam question where the "management arrangement" is casual, oral, or missing deposit language — assume trouble.

A licensee dealing in property **as a principal** must notify the broker **in writing** before listing, selling, buying, exchanging, renting, leasing, or optioning property in the licensee's own name or through an entity where the licensee has an ownership or officer/employee/member role. If the licensee is buying listed property or selling owned property, the licensee must **clearly disclose that status and insert a clause in the contract**. In practice, this means a "side deal" done without the broker's prior written knowledge is exactly the kind of conduct the state tests and disciplines.

Advertising questions are extremely testable in Georgia:
- A licensee may not advertise real estate without the **written permission** of the owner or authorized agent.
- If the property is listed with **another firm**, the ad must clearly and conspicuously disclose that fact **and the listing firm's name**, unless that listing firm has waived the disclosure in writing.
- **Internet ads** must disclose the licensee's **firm name and telephone number on every viewable webpage** of the site, subject to the rule's narrow linking exceptions for limited-character or third-party platforms.

GREC's own newsletter gives a perfect real-world enforcement example: a salesperson posted two homes listed by another firm on the salesperson's own website **without permission**, did not submit the content for broker review, and omitted the broker's name/phone and the listing broker's name. GREC reports that the salesperson was cited and assessed money for the violation.

## Trust Funds, Transaction Handling, and the Steps That Get Tested

Georgia's trust-account rules are central:

- Brokers may maintain more than one designated trust or escrow account, but each account must be **registered with the Commission within one month** of opening.
- A licensee must place cash, checks, or other items of value received in a brokerage capacity into the custody of the broker **as soon after receipt as is practicably possible**.
- Unless the parties agree otherwise in writing, the broker holding the funds must **promptly deposit** them into a **federally insured account designated as a trust account and registered with GREC**.
- If the broker uses an **interest-bearing trust account**, the broker must first obtain a **written agreement saying who gets the interest**.
- All trust accounts must be registered, are subject to examination, and any interest allocation must be backed by a written agreement.

Transaction-handling rules create a long list of exam "musts":
- A licensee must **promptly tender** any signed offer to the customer or client.
- If the offeror is not the licensee's own client/customer, the licensee receiving the offer must provide a copy to the licensee working with or representing the offeree.
- Brokerage engagements and offers must include the **six-digit Georgia license number** of each participating firm and each participating licensee.
- Anyone who signs a document in the transaction must receive a copy.
- Brokers involved in the transaction must retain required transaction documents for **three years**, available to GREC on reasonable request.
- No licensee may falsify a transaction document or knowingly misstate the true price.

The **timing of brokerage disclosures** is especially important. GREC Rule 520-1-.06 requires written disclosure to both parties of whom the firm represents — or whether the firm is a dual agent — **and from whom the firm will receive valuable consideration** — **no later than the time any party first makes an offer**. Separately, the Brokerage Relationships Act states that all brokerage engagements must advise the prospective client of the types of agency relationships available through the broker. Georgia tests both the **content** of the disclosure and the **deadline** for giving it.

## Finance, Closing, and Foreclosure as They Play Out in Georgia

For mortgage closings, the CFPB's "Know Before You Owe" framework matters. The **Closing Disclosure** is a **five-page** form containing final loan terms, projected monthly payments, and closing costs. **The consumer must receive it at least three business days before closing.** If an exam question says the buyer got final loan numbers one day before closing and signed anyway — recognize that as a federal closing-disclosure timing problem, not just a customer-service issue.

In Georgia practice, real-estate closings are **attorney-driven**. The Georgia Supreme Court's Formal Advisory Opinion 13-1 states that, unless parties are handling the transaction within Georgia's pro se exemption, **Georgia law requires that a lawyer handle a real-estate closing**. A lawyer **may not** ethically conduct a "witness only" closing, and closing funds received by the lawyer must be deposited into and disbursed from the lawyer's trust account.

A practical Georgia **closing workflow**:

```
1. Brokerage engagement signed
2. Agency relationship disclosed
3. Offer accepted and copies distributed
4. Earnest money delivered to broker
5. Trust funds deposited into registered trust account
6. Lender issues Loan Estimate and later Closing Disclosure
7. Closing attorney / title work and final settlement prep
8. Buyer receives Closing Disclosure at least 3 business days before closing
9. Signing, funding, deed/security deed execution
10. Recording and post-closing disbursement
```

For **foreclosure**, Georgia law centers on sales under a **power of sale**:
- Notice of initiation of proceedings to exercise the power of sale must be given to the debtor **no later than 30 days** before the proposed foreclosure date.
- If the creditor later wants a **deficiency judgment**, Georgia requires a **judicial confirmation/approval process** after the foreclosure sale.
- Georgia law expressly refers to the "power of nonjudicial foreclosure sale," which is why Georgia foreclosure questions often test sale procedure rather than a full courtroom foreclosure action.

Georgia foreclosure workflow:

```
1. Borrower default under security deed
2. Secured creditor sends foreclosure notice
3. Notice sent at least 30 days before proposed sale
4. Sale conducted under power of sale as required by statute
5. Deed under power filed
6. Does lender seek deficiency?
   - No: process ends after sale
   - Yes: lender seeks judicial confirmation and approval
```

For exam math, keep these formulas ready even when the question is wrapped in a Georgia fact pattern:

- **Loan-to-value** = loan amount ÷ property value
- **Discount points cost** = loan amount × point percentage
- **Simple interest** = principal × rate × time
- **PITI** = principal + interest + taxes + insurance

These are general math tools rather than Georgia-specific legal rules, but they are the fastest way through finance-and-closing questions.

## Sample Exam-Style Questions with Answers

**Q:** A seller signs an exclusive listing with no end date because the parties "will know when the property is sold." Is that compliant?
**A:** **No.** In Georgia, each exclusive brokerage agreement must fully set forth its terms and have a **definite expiration date**.

**Q:** A salesperson receives a buyer's earnest-money check on Friday evening and leaves it in a desk drawer until Tuesday because the office is closed for the weekend. What is the best answer?
**A:** The licensee must place the check into the broker's custody **as soon after receipt as is practicably possible**, and unless otherwise agreed in writing, the broker must **promptly deposit** it into a registered trust account.

**Q:** When must written disclosure of whom the firm represents — or whether the firm is a dual agent — be made?
**A:** In a timely manner, but **no later than the time any party first makes an offer** to purchase, sell, lease, or exchange real property.

**Q:** A salesperson advertises another firm's listing on a website and leaves off the listing firm's name because "everyone can click through to it." Is that okay?
**A:** **No**, unless the listing firm has expressly waived the disclosure in writing. Georgia requires written authority to advertise and, when advertising a property listed with another firm, clear disclosure that it is listed with another firm and the listing firm's name. Internet ads must also show the licensee's firm name and phone on every viewable page, subject to limited exceptions.

**Q:** How long must transaction records such as sales contracts, brokerage engagements, closing statements, and leases be maintained?
**A:** **Three years** in the broker's file where required by law.

**Q:** A licensee wants to buy a listed property personally. What must the licensee do?
**A:** Disclose the licensee's position as buyer to the seller **and** insert a clause to that effect in the contract; if the licensee is acting as principal, the licensee must also **advise the broker in writing** before acting.

## Real-Life Scenario Questions with Answers

**Scenario:** A broker wants to keep earnest money uncashed "until we know the deal is solid."
**Answer:** That is not the default Georgia rule. Unless the parties agree otherwise in writing, the broker holding funds must **promptly deposit** them in a federally insured, designated trust account registered with GREC.

**Scenario:** A buyer receives the Closing Disclosure the day before a mortgage closing and finds the cash-to-close amount is much higher than expected.
**Answer:** The consumer should flag the issue immediately because the Closing Disclosure is the final federal closing form and must be provided **at least three business days before closing**. That timeline exists so buyers can compare it to the Loan Estimate and resolve problems before signing.

**Scenario:** A lender forecloses and then sues the borrower for the unpaid balance remaining afterward.
**Answer:** In Georgia, if the lender wants a deficiency judgment after a foreclosure sale under power of sale, the lender must pursue the statutory **confirmation and approval process**.
